EXPERIENCE THE MAGICAL FESTIVAL IN DENMARK

Step into a world of music, lights, and Nordic charm as Denmark’s festivals bring streets and shores to life. From cozy hygge moments to vibrant cultural celebrations, every festival is a memory waiting to happen .





Sankt Hans Festival

The evening of June 23rd

The evening is a combined celebration of Saint John the Baptist, who was supposedly born on 24 June, and a celebration of the shortest night of the tear.The evening is traditionally celebrated with bonfires, speeches and singing of the Danish midsummer song “Vi Elsker Vort Land” (We Love Our Country), which was written by Danish poet Holger Drachmann in 1885.

Distortion: A week of orchestrated street chaos

Late May/ start June

If you're visiting Copenhagen in late May/start June, you need make a choice. You can either stay away from the neighbourhoods of Nørrebro and Vesterbro or join us and thousands of other Danes at one of Europe's largest street parties! Not such a difficult choice after all, huh? Because Distortion is an over-the-top, mobile party-extravaganza that's surely not to be missed.

Easter in Denmark (Påske)

Hoppy times at Easter Or Påske, as we call it, brings not only great food and festivities, but weird and wonderful traditions too. Less of a religious holiday these days, it’s more focused on welcoming spring, celebrating with family or friends and injecting some colour into our usually toned-down homes.

New Year's Eve in Denmark

If you're lucky enough to spend New Year's Eve in Denmark, you can look forward to watching the King's New Year's Speech on television at 18:00 - don't miss it! All of Denmark tunes in to hear its monarch's statement about the year that has passed and the challenges of the year ahead. The New Year's Speech has been a national rallying point since 1942, when the much-admired King Christian X offered hope and encouragement to a country in the grip of a German occupation.

Fastelavn Traditions in Denmark

Sunday or Monday before Ash Wednesday

Taking place seven weeks prior to Easter, the main tradition associated with the holiday was the placing of a black cat in a barrel and beating the barrel with fastelavnsris (this can be anything from a stick or a club or a bunch of twigs) until it broke apart, releasing the (probably traumatized) cat. This was done to ward off evil.
